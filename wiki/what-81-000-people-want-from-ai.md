# What 81,000 People Want from AI

## Summary

Anthropic's April 2026 report on a large-scale qualitative study conducted in December 2025, in which **80,508 people** across **159 countries** and **70 languages** held structured conversational interviews with **[[Anthropic Interviewer]]** — a Claude instance prompted to conduct open-ended qualitative interviews — about how they use AI, what they hope for, and what they fear. Anthropic positions it as the largest and most multilingual qualitative study ever conducted. [[Claude]]-powered classifiers coded responses across "what people want" (single-label), concerns (multi-label), occupation, and overall sentiment; representative quotes were extracted with Claude and human-reviewed for privacy. The central finding is that **hope and alarm coexist within each individual** rather than dividing the population into camps, and that the top hope — "Professional excellence" — is named by 18.8% of respondents as their primary aspiration for AI.

## Key Claims

- **Scale**: 80,508 interviews across 159 countries, 70 languages, one week in December 2025. Conducted via Claude.ai opt-in; de-identified before analysis.
- **Method**: Anthropic Interviewer asked a fixed question set with adaptive follow-ups, bridging the usual depth-vs-scale tradeoff in qualitative research. Responses were classified by Claude and manually reviewed; methods documented in a published Appendix.
- **Top primary hopes** (single-label, first two surfaced):
  - **Professional excellence** — 18.8% — lean into strategic / meaningful work by offloading routine tasks.
  - **Personal transformation** — 13.7% — personal growth, health, learning, relationships.
- **Qualitative frame**: "hope and alarm don't divide people into camps, so much as coexist as tensions within each person" — e.g. a lawyer using AI to review contracts who simultaneously fears losing the ability to read unaided.
- **Concrete benefits cited**: diagnosis after 9 years of misdiagnosis (US freelancer); income-cycle break (Nigeria entrepreneur); reduced documentation burden restoring patience with colleagues (US healthcare worker).
- **Concrete harms cited**: job displacement (US technical support specialist laid off in May when employer substituted AI); broad anxiety about "something smarter than itself" (SK software engineer).
- **Framing posture**: the report is explicitly a counter to abstract risk/benefit discourse — it grounds "AI going well" in the concrete aspirations of actual users, not projected scenarios.
