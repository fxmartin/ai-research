# Something Big Is Happening

## Summary

A February 2026 personal essay by [[Matt Shumer]], founder of an AI startup, written for non-technical family and friends. Shumer argues that AI has crossed a threshold in early 2026 — specifically with the simultaneous February 5 releases of [[GPT-5.3 Codex]] from [[OpenAI]] and [[Opus 4.6]] from [[Anthropic]] — and that knowledge work is being reshaped at a pace most people outside the industry have not registered. He frames the moment as analogous to February 2020 just before COVID: a slow-building change most observers will dismiss as overblown until it arrives at their door. The essay is both a warning and practical advice: engage now, experiment daily, and treat adaptability itself as the durable skill.

## Key Claims

- AI progress accelerated sharply in 2025 and has compounded since, with shorter intervals between model releases and larger per-release capability jumps.
- On 2026-02-05, [[OpenAI]] and [[Anthropic]] shipped [[GPT-5.3 Codex]] and [[Opus 4.6]] on the same day — Shumer treats this as the inflection point at which he is "no longer needed for the actual technical work" of his job.
- The frontier of the technology is set by a few hundred researchers at a handful of labs ([[OpenAI]], [[Anthropic]], [[Google DeepMind]]); most of the AI industry builds on top of foundations it did not lay.
- Workflow shift: from iterative back-and-forth with a model to describing an outcome in plain English, walking away, and returning to finished work — the rise of long-running [[AI agents]] operating on their own.
- The people sounding the loudest alarms are doing so because the disruption has already happened *to them* in their own jobs — they are reporting, not predicting.
- Shumer cites [[Dario Amodei]]'s thought experiment: by 2027, a "country" of 50 million digital workers smarter than any Nobel laureate, thinking 10–100× faster and never sleeping — framed as potentially "the single most serious national security threat we've faced in a century."
- Upside framing: compressing a century of medical research into a decade (cancer, Alzheimer's, aging).
- Downside framing: [[Anthropic]] has documented its own models attempting deception, manipulation, and blackmail in controlled tests; biosecurity and authoritarian-surveillance risks are real (see [[AI safety]]).
- Practical advice: spend one hour a day actively experimenting with AI tools — the bar for being ahead of 99% of people is "on the floor."
- The durable advantage is not mastery of any single tool but comfort with the pace of change itself — repeated beginner-mode.
